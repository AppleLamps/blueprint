Tonight marks the first night of Passover, the Jewish holiday celebrating the exodus of Israelites from slavery in Egypt. Since I was often the youngest child at the Seder table, it usually fell on me to recite the Four Questions that begin with the familiar *Ma Nishtana*, which translates to “Why is this night different from all other nights?”

While Hanukkah was most Jewish children’s favorite holiday, in my household, it was always Pesach. I was captivated by the plagues, terrified by the image of enslaved Jews forced to make bricks without straw, and awed by the parting of the Red Sea. I took pride in Moses standing up to Pharaoh with the powerful words: “Let my people go,” and I found hope in proclaiming at the end of the meal, “Next year in Jerusalem!”

But most of all, I loved the feast — sitting around the table with family, eating special foods reserved just for this night, partaking in matzah and enjoying strange desserts made without flour. For a kid growing up in New York in the 1970s and '80s, it was joyful and exotic.

As an adult, I came to understand the broader significance of Passover. It’s the only Jewish holiday that aligns with the Christian calendar — it was, after all, the setting for Jesus’ Last Supper. And for many communities, including Black Americans, the story of the Exodus has come to symbolize a universal narrative of liberation from oppression.

This year, the holiday holds added meaning for me and many others. It is the story of a person willing to risk himself and his community to stand up to a tyrannical ruler.

The story of Moses teaches us that it's okay to suffer doubt and to question whether our efforts will succeed or fail. Even the best leaders make mistakes and are at times overcome by emotion. What matters is remaining steadfast in our commitment to what is right and having the courage to confront the powerful.

This weekend, whether you're celebrating Passover, preparing for Easter or simply taking time to relax, consider what you can do to stand up for what you know is right. It might be something as small as posting on social media. It might mean attending a peaceful protest in your community. It might even be standing up to an aspiring dictator and making clear: we are not afraid and won’t back down.

Most importantly, don’t let the scale of the challenge stop you from taking the first step.
