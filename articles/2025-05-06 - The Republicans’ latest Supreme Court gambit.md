It’s only Tuesday, and already this week Donald Trump has said he doesn’t know if, as president, he must uphold the U.S. Constitution and has suggested imposing tariffs on movies. One reason it’s difficult to cover politics and the courts in this era is that Trump so frequently breaks the law and violates the Constitution that it is hard to keep track of everything else threatening democracy.

But every so often, we need to turn down the volume on Trump and pay attention to what his allies are doing to subvert democracy. When we do, we inevitably hear the low roar of the Republican voter suppression machine grinding away in the courts.

Even when the GOP is defeated in lower courts, Republicans routinely bring their fanciful claims and novel legal theories to the U.S. Supreme Court. We all remember how, in December 2020, Trump made a last-ditch attempt to have the Court overturn the election results. It failed.

So did the Republican effort to strip state courts of the power to enforce their own constitutions through the radical fringe “independent state legislature theory.” Most recently, Republicans failed to gut the Voting Rights Act in a case involving Alabama’s congressional districts.

But the fact that the GOP keeps losing doesn’t mean it has given up. Republicans understand that all it takes is persuading the conservative majority on the Supreme Court in one critical case to profoundly impact free and fair elections.

Savvy observers of law and politics know that even the most frivolous legal theory can gain traction — with dire consequences. They may be long-shots, but here are the current anti-voting lawsuits the right-wing is pitching to the Supreme Court for review:

### Pennsylvania Ballot Curing Challenge

The RNC and Pennsylvania Republican Party continue their obsession with disqualifying ballots in Pennsylvania. In this case, they are invoking the failed independent state legislature theory to overturn a 2024 Pennsylvania Supreme Court ruling. That decision allowed voters to cast provisional ballots on Election Day if their timely submitted mail-in ballots were disqualified due to a technical error. The RNC claims the state court “transgressed” the ordinary bounds of judicial review and “usurped” the authority of the Pennsylvania Legislature.

This is one of the most important cases to watch.

### Michigan Voting Rights Constitutional Amendments Challenge

Republican state legislators are asking the Supreme Court to review their challenge to two pro-voting constitutional amendments recently enshrined in the Michigan Constitution. A district court dismissed the case, and the 6th Circuit upheld that dismissal. Yet, the GOP is asking the Court to decide if individual state legislators can bring Election Clause claims against the executive branch, even though the legislature — then controlled by Democrats — did not challenge the amendments when they passed.

This is a longshot, but one that would harm voting in Michigan.

### Virginia Felony Disenfranchisement Challenge

Pro-voting groups are using a novel legal theory to challenge Virginia’s constitutional provision that disenfranchises people with felony convictions. The plaintiffs argue that this provision violates the Virginia Readmission Act of 1870, which only permits the state to deny the right to vote to those convicted of “common law” felonies as defined in 1870. Members of the Virginia State Board of Elections are trying to halt the case by asking the U.S. Supreme Court to determine whether courts can enforce the Readmission Act.

This is a very interesting case involving an arcane area of the law. Yet, for people with felony convictions, the consequences are very real.

### Illinois Ballot Receipt Deadline

Illinois Republican Rep. Michael Bost has asked the U.S. Supreme Court to review his challenge to the state’s ballot receipt deadline. Both a federal district court and the 7th Circuit dismissed the case for lack of standing, as Bost and two GOP presidential elector nominees failed to show any harm caused by the state’s 14-day ballot receipt window. Bost now argues that the courts erred and is asking SCOTUS to rule that he and the other plaintiffs may challenge the state law.

I would be shocked if the court took this case, but it shows the Republican’s continued obsession with mail ballot receipt deadlines.
