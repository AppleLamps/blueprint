Firings in the federal government have dominated the news recently. Since taking office, Donald Trump’s administration has fired government inspectors general, federal prosecutors, FBI agents and national security officials. It has pressured many others to resign or face the consequences. It recently made a near government-wide offer to career civil servants — get months of paid severance to leave your employment now.

While Republicans have looked the other way, this government purge has otherwise been widely condemned as an affront to the constitution and rule of law. Critics have pointed out the unfairness of firing career officials for merely following the law and established policy. They have rightfully worried about what the loss of expertise will mean for the smooth operations of the government moving forward.

But Trump is not trying to shrink the size of the federal government’s workforce to save money. He is not a fiscal hawk or a conservative. As Steve Bannon, the keeper of MAGA’s core ideology, recently said about himself; “I’m a hard-core populist. I’m a hard-core nationalist. I’m not a conservative. I think conservatives are [expletive].”

The most dangerous part of Trump’s plan for the federal workforce is not who leaves, it is who comes in. Call it Trump’s great replacement theory. If successful, it has the potential to weaponize the government against his political opponents and undermine our democracy for years to come. And it is already underway.

When he became president, Ronald Reagan adopted the maxim that “personnel is policy.” Conservatives of that era understood that even the most powerful president could only extend his reach so far. To ensure the administration’s policies were implemented, it was critical to put the right people in places of authority.

Back then, it was carried out through political appointees that shared Reagan’s brand of anti-Washington conservatism. It meant appointing tax-cutting conservatives and defense hawks to key posts. It meant a Department of Justice that focused on drug crimes and an interior department that exploited, rather than preserved, public lands.

The problem with Trump is that he has no governing philosophy. He will adopt and change policies based on what he believes is best for him. He has no allies he won’t betray. He has no convictions he won’t reverse. For him all politics is personal and thus personnel is personal.

We have seen this in his early cabinet nominations. Pam Bondi was chosen to head the Department of Justice because she proved a loyal election denier with a track record of saying and doing what Trump wants. Kash Patel is such a sycophant that he published a book that literally contained an enemies list as well as children’s books in which Trump is the King.

He found the one man on Wall Street willing to shill for his economy-killing tariffs. He appointed Marco Rubio secretary of state because he had proven himself willing to say or do anything Trump asked. In a court of jesters, Little Marco was the most eager to humiliate himself for Trump’s pleasure.

This would be bad enough if Trump was satisfied with a band of misfits running departments and agencies while supplicant billionaires paid for the privilege of bowing down and begging for Trump’s attention and approval. But it is worse. Much worse.

To execute his planned revenge, he needs the cooperation of FBI agents willing to disregard their oaths. To prosecute his enemies, he needs line prosecutors eager to disregard legal ethics and the evidence to indict his political opponents. To be the authoritarian he aspires to be, he needs all branches of the federal government — large and small — to reward his supporters and punish his detractors.

This is why he wants good prosecutors to resign. This is why he wants to fire FBI agents who follow the law. Because, to execute his reign of vengeance, he needs a federal workforce that is willing to carry out his orders.

Some, particularly those who stay, will be motivated by fear of losing their jobs. They will compromise themselves little by little. Others, who fill the vacancies, will be eager to display loyalty and fealty to the man who they think can aid their careers. But most will be the silent so-called good people that Martin Luthur King Jr. warned us about. They will justify their immoral actions by saying they were just following orders.

There will be some who resist — who insist on following the law and serving the American people rather than a one-man dictatorship. We have already seen examples of such heroism in the FBI, Treasury and USAID. Over time, however, the righteous will be weeded out or worn down.

We are at a critical moment in this fight for democracy. Trump’s great replacement has started but it has not taken root. It is now that it must be defeated. That will require civic courage by those in government and outside.

For those in government, stand tall and do not give into illegal orders or actions. For those outside of government, we must shine a light on these illegal actions every moment of every day. I expect little from the oligarchs and their media enablers. Independent media will necessarily have to shoulder the load, as it has already proven itself capable of doing.

Most importantly, the legal profession needs to step forward to fight against these illegal efforts. Already groups like Democracy Forward are suing and winning. But they cannot do this alone. Big law firms need to pitch in, rather than hide.

Finally, the courts will once again need to protect the Constitution and rule of law from a lawless president who has ignored his oath once again. So far, they have held. Trump is losing in court. For democracy’s sake, let’s hope that continues.

Here are my tips for the week.

###### **NEW LAWSUITS**

### **Democracy in court**

If you want to doom scroll, pay attention to what Elon Musk is doing. If you want to get angry, pay attention to OMB and the White House. But, if you want to know what is happening to democracy, pay attention to the courts. There will be a lot of litigation this week across the democracy spectrum — from Trump’s executive orders to agency actions, to voting and elections. Get used to courts being the central players for the next few years — at least.

###### **TRUMP’S CABINET**

### **Senate nominations and confirmations**

I have been on record saying that every Trump nominee will be confirmed. There may be a stray Republican here or there that votes no — but never enough of them to defeat one. This is a well-practiced game in Washington, D.C. — allowing a vulnerable Republican the ability to look independent from Trump while knowing it won’t make a difference. Though Gabbard looks to be in trouble, I am sticking to my initial prediction.

###### **MUSK IN CHARGE**

### **Congress shall make no law**

I cannot remember a president who came into office with majorities in both Houses of Congress who had zero legislative agenda. So far Speaker Johnson and Leader Thune are mere props as Trump ignores the role of Congress to, well, do anything at all. With Elon Musk seemingly playing the role of appropriator in chief, it is shocking to see Congress have zero interest in how their spending bills are being ignored. Expect this to continue.
