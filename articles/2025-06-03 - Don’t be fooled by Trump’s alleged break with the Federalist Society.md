The sudden falling out between Donald Trump and the Federalist Society is a ruse. Don’t be fooled. Trump is doing what he always does: creating a permission structure within the GOP to seize power and undermine the rule of law. We must not let him succeed.

It is no secret that Trump wants to be a dictator. Over the course of his first months in office, he used his position to claim powers he did not have, purge career civil servants and wage a campaign of retribution and intimidation against his opponents.

Standing in his way has been the courts. Federal judges have not blocked all his actions, but they have proven to be the biggest obstacle to his illegal and unconstitutional power grab. In May alone, Trump and his administration lost over 95% of the cases decided by federal district courts.

Trump has noticed. In recent weeks, the administration’s rhetoric toward the judiciary has become increasingly threatening. Senior officials, including the vice president, have floated the idea that they might not have to comply with court orders. Department of Justice lawyers have become noticeably dismissive and even rude toward the judges they appear before. Judges are increasingly concerned for their physical safety.

Trump is not just losing cases before judges nominated by other presidents. He is losing before judges he selected — judges he vouched for and insisted would apply the law faithfully and uphold the Constitution.

Indeed, in his first administration, nominating conservative judges was a priority. Filling a record number of judicial vacancies with young, rock-ribbed conservatives was something he and his team bragged about. To accomplish this monumental task, Trump turned to the Federalist Society and its hyper-conservative leader, Leonard Leo.

Leo is not just any conservative. A friend of several of the most conservative Supreme Court justices, he made it his life’s mission to transform the federal judiciary into a reliable tool for a far-right agenda. Under his leadership, the Federalist Society became a well-oiled machine capable of identifying the most conservative rising lawyers and channeling them into influential positions in government — particularly the judiciary

When Don McGahn, Trump’s first White House counsel, was accused of outsourcing judicial selections to the Federalist Society, he demurred: “I’ve been a member of the Federalist Society since law school. Still am, so frankly it seems like it’s been in-sourced.” Continuing, McGahn clarified: “Seeking advice from Leonard Leo and many members of the Federalist Society is not outsourcing the judicial selection process. The fact is, we all share the same vision of the judicial role, and we welcome input from many sources.”

Now, in his second term, Trump wants it known that he does not, in fact, share the same vision for judges as Leo or his group. Trump claims he is done with the Federalist Society and Leo, whom he now calls a “bad person” and a “real sleazebag.”

The immediate reaction from the mainstream media — and even some on the left — was to categorize the rift as a genuine parting of ways. Some pointed to the Federalist Society’s lack of enthusiasm for Trump’s election denialism and the events of Jan. 6. Others claimed the former president was upset that the group was not more loyal during his criminal cases. A few even suggested it was related to Trump’s recent appointment of his henchman, Emile Bove, to the 3rd Circuit.

Each of these explanations gives both the Federalist Society and Trump more credit than they deserve.

While there are, no doubt, some members of the Federalist Society who disagreed with Trump’s efforts to overturn an election, incite an insurrection and lie about it, this was certainly not a rallying cry among its faithful. On the contrary, many prominent conservative lawyers who were outraged by Trump left the Federalist Society and formed a competing group — the Society for the Rule of Law Institute — precisely because of the Federalist Society’s continued loyalty to Trump.

By picking a fight with the Federalist Society over judges, Trump is moving the goalposts for who is considered an acceptable judicial nominee — forcing Republicans, particularly Senate Republicans, to embrace his new standard. It is no coincidence that on the same day Trump announced his disdain for the Federalist Society, his sycophantic Attorney General, Pam Bondi, sent a letter to the American Bar Association announcing it would no longer cooperate in the ABA’s process for rating judicial nominees.

Trump has made it clear that the only test for a judicial nominee is their professed loyalty to him. He is not interested in a prospective judge’s judicial philosophy or even their qualifications. All he wants to know is whether they will rule in his favor 100% of the time. If they will, he will nominate them. If not, there’s the door.

Complicating this calculation does a disservice to litigants who will appear before these future judges and to those who most rely on the rule of law. It also obscures the genuine threat to the Constitution and democracy posed by Trump and his future nominees.

We cannot allow Trump to normalize a nominating process based on loyalty to him and his agenda. We cannot permit ourselves to find common ground where subversion of the rule of law is the only common ground Trump will accept. We must oppose Trump and his judicial nominees as if democracy is at stake — because it is.

As for Leo and the Federalist Society, they will soon accommodate Trump’s latest demands and likely find a way to pay for the privilege of humiliating themselves before him. They are neither heroes nor victims of Trump. They are only enablers and collaborators.

Those of us in the pro-democracy movement cannot be fooled, must not forget and must continue to fight.
