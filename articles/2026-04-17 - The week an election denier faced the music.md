The week an election denier faced the music5:30One of the first lessons I learned as a lawyer is that no one wants to have a legal problem. Hiring a lawyer, like going to the doctor, is born of necessity. Just as patients do not seek out interesting medical illnesses, clients do not celebrate fascinating legal problems.

When it comes to voting and election cases, too many lawyers and political observers forget this. The object is to enable voters to cast ballots under a fair set of rules and have those ballots counted. Lawyers may celebrate or pick apart the legal reasoning and the procedural developments, but the clients want to be able to vote.

I reflected on this as I read the analysis of a case my law firm won this week in Indiana. Last year, the state legislature removed college IDs from the accepted list of voter identification. On Tuesday, a federal judge agreed that our client was likely to prevail on the merits in his challenge to the law, and blocked it.

What happened next depends on where you stand.

Legal analysts point out that Indiana immediately appealed the ruling to the conservative 7th Circuit Court of Appeals. In their view, our victory remains incomplete and very much in doubt.

Yet, from the standpoint of our client — and many like him — it looks very different. On Wednesday morning, he voted using his college ID.

No matter what happens to the case from here, from his perspective, the case was a success. As a result of an Indiana law, he faced disenfranchisement. Because of our lawsuit, he instead cast his ballot.

The next time someone tries to sell you on a case as a victory or defeat, tell them you don't want to know what a law professor thinks — you want to know whether the client believes they prevailed.

That is how I judge my court wins and losses. And on that score, Indiana was a complete success.

## What worried me most this week

Thursday brought bad news from North Carolina. The state’s Republican-controlled board of elections announced it is [sharing](https://www.democracydocket.com/news-alerts/north-carolina-election-boards-gop-majority-approves-sharing-voter-data-with-dhs/) its unredacted voter data with the Trump administration.

North Carolina claims it will use the Department of Homeland Security’s SAVE database to check voter rolls. The immigration database is decades old, and notoriously inaccurate. It has been known to erroneously flag U.S. citizens as noncitizens.

This is both a safety concern and a voter suppression tool. The Trump administration has proven to be careless when it comes to checking for immigration status. The use of this system in North Carolina will surely put U.S. citizens in harm's way.

It will also delay eligible voters who were inaccurately flagged. Voters may not be aware that their registration has been affected until they show up at the polls. If they are informed, it may be too late to re-register.

This is just the latest example of the GOP-controlled North Carolina election board taking steps to disenfranchise voters. Though I am worried, North Carolina should know I am also watching.

## What I'm watching in the courts

Louisiana is [suing](https://www.democracydocket.com/news-alerts/louisiana-sues-key-federal-election-agency-to-implement-its-anti-voting-law/) the U.S. Election Assistance Commission (EAC) for blocking part of the state’s restrictive proof of citizenship law. Louisiana passed its anti-voting law in 2024, but the EAC split on whether to approve adding new state-specific language requiring citizenship documents to register to the federal voter registration form. Without a majority, the request was denied.

Louisiana isn’t letting it go. While Louisiana claims the EAC is violating the National Voter Registration Act (NVRA), its motivation is much simpler: The Republican-controlled state is trying to disenfranchise voters.

As I have pointed out before, proving citizenship is not easy. Many people don’t have passports — and if the document is expired, it’s not simple or inexpensive to get a new one. Plenty of people don’t have easy access to their original birth certificate. And the problems facing women who change their legal names when they are married are well-documented.

Republicans in Louisiana aren’t blind to these hurdles. In fact, they are taking advantage of them. That’s why my firm was already fighting hard to protect the voters in Louisiana who would be affected by this law. Keep an eye on this new case — it’s an important one.

## What gave me hope this week

John Eastman has been [disbarred](https://www.democracydocket.com/news-alerts/key-trump-lawyer-who-tried-to-overturn-2020-election-permanently-disbarred/).

Eastman was a key architect in the failed attempt to overturn the 2020 election. The former Trump lawyer wrote a series of memos outlining ways to keep Trump in power after the election — including the infamous debunked legal theory suggesting that then-Vice President Mike Pence could reject electoral votes from key battleground states.

Pence rejected the plan, and it ultimately culminated in the Jan. 6 attack on the Capitol.

Shortly after Jan. 6, 2021, I wrote about the need for accountability for the lawyers involved in undermining the 2020 election. At long last, the California Supreme Court agreed — declining to intervene after lower courts found Eastman advanced baseless claims to help Trump cling to power.

Other election deniers and sycophants serving Trump should take note. There are consequences for their actions, and Trump will not save them in the end. I have hope that others — from the last administration to this one — will be held accountable for the damage they have done to our democracy.
