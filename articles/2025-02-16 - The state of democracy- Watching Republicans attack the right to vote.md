With the flurry of executive orders and the chaos Elon Musk and others are creating, it is easy to lose sight of the fact that the only consistent through line to Donald Trump’s politics is election denialism and voter suppression.

Tariffs are negotiable, government agencies can come and go, even political enemies can even score pardons if they offer the right deal. But no one — I mean no one — can be in good standing in Trump’s GOP if they do not insist on denying the outcome of elections he has lost and advocate for suppressing the voting rights of citizens who did not vote for him. Period.

That is why Republicans in Congress have prioritized enacting the so-called SAVE Act. The Safeguard American Voter Eligibility Act would seriously undermine voting rights under the false pretense of preventing nearly non-existent instances of noncitizen voting in federal elections.

Among its core provisions, it would require those seeking to register to vote to provide documentary proof of citizenship. For most people born in the United States this would require either a passport or certified birth certificate.

![1-required-to-vote](https://member.democracydocket.com/hs-fs/hubfs/1-required-to-vote.png?width=1200&height=444&name=1-required-to-vote.png)

Plenty of commentators have noted the difficulties this would pose for younger and less affluent voters who more often lack passports. Others have noted the discrimination married women who adopt their husband’s surname will face, since their birth certificate name will not match their married name. And, of course, there is the fact that this will make voter registration other than in person in a government office either very difficult or impossible.

Simply put, the SAVE Act is a voter suppression bill aimed at making it harder for many Democratic constituencies to vote and easier for the GOP to cheat. But, under Trump’s scheme, that isn’t the worst of it.

Among his first executive orders was purporting to end birthright citizenship — the right to automatic citizenship for any person born in the United States regardless of the status of their parents. Notwithstanding the clear text of the U.S. Constitution, Trump has claimed the power to replace birthright citizenship with a more restrictive regime whereby many people born in the U.S. would not be citizens if their parents were not.

![2-Trumps-first-EOs](https://member.democracydocket.com/hs-fs/hubfs/2-Trumps-first-EOs.jpeg?width=2500&height=1259&name=2-Trumps-first-EOs.jpeg)

The result for voting would be clear. U.S. birth certificates would no longer serve as conclusive proof of citizenship. Citizens seeking to register to vote would lose a critical piece of documentary proof of citizenship needed to satisfy the requirements of the SAVE Act, and state laws like it.

This is no oversight by Trump and the GOP. Several of the lawsuits challenging Trump’s executive order make exactly this point. Yet, the Trump administration has offered no assurances that its unconstitutional effort to strip citizenship would not ultimately be a tool to unconstitutionally strip citizens of the right to vote.

![3-no-birth-certificate-no-voting](https://member.democracydocket.com/hs-fs/hubfs/3-no-birth-certificate-no-voting.png?width=1200&height=444&name=3-no-birth-certificate-no-voting.png)

But that isn’t the only action Trump took to undermine voting in advance of the 2026 elections. He also rescinded Joe Biden’s 2021 pro-voting executive order that promoted increasing voter registrations nationwide. And he promptly put on leave employees within the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency whose jobs are helping to secure elections from foreign threats and disinformation.

Meanwhile, the Republican Party is continuing to push its anti-voting agenda in court. The RNC is again asking the U.S. Supreme Court to hear a case involving the fringe independent state legislature theory, this time out of Pennsylvania. You may recall, the Republican Attorney General in Montana recently failed in a similar effort involving a series of voter suppression laws struck down in that state.

Perhaps worst of all, Republican state Supreme Court candidate Jefferson Griffin is still trying to steal an election in North Carolina by throwing out more than 60,000 lawful votes. A state trial court recently [**rejected**](https://www.democracydocket.com/news-alerts/north-carolina-judge-rejects-gop-state-supreme-court-candidates-bid-to-throw-out-60k-ballots/) his challenge, but he has since appealed that decision.

Finally, while most eyes are on Washington, DC, Republican legislatures around the country are meeting to consider new draconian anti-voting laws. Here are just a few examples.

- The Wisconsin state legislature has [**approved**](https://apnews.com/article/wisconsin-voter-id-election-photo-f6a74b9c83ef0fb147e67489a9df1274) a ballot measure to enshrine a voter ID requirement in the state constitution. The proposed amendment will appear on the ballot for the April 1 election.
- Similarly, Maine Republicans are pushing a [**voter ID constitutional amendment**](https://voteridforme.com/files/photo_id_for_voting_petition_final.pdf) that would make sweeping changes to election procedure. In addition to voter ID requirements, the proposal would restrict absentee ballot processes, limit drop-box use, and introduce other hurdles to the right to vote.
- The North Carolina state legislature is [**considering**](https://www.carolinajournal.com/new-bill-would-reduce-early-voting-days-in-nc/) a bill to shorten the state’s early voting period from 17 days to 8 days, while [**Alaska Republicans**](https://gov.alaska.gov/governor-dunleavy-introduces-three-key-bills-to-advance-alaskas-priorities-on-day-two-of-legislative-session/) are working to slash the last five days of early voting before election day.
- The Wyoming state legislature considered a bill that would have banned the use of electronic voting machines. It would’ve made Wyoming the only state in the nation to require hand-counting all ballots.
- In order to circumvent Governor Hobbs, Arizona lawmakers are [**rapidly advancing**](https://www.votebeat.org/arizona/2025/02/12/election-results-speed-faster-bill-compromise-katie-hobbs-veto-threat/) a ballot measure that restricts ballot return and creates onerous requirements to remain on the Active Early Voter List.
- Indiana lawmakers are pushing bills that would make it harder to [**register**](https://indianacapitalchronicle.com/2025/01/28/elections-bill-would-strike-down-student-ids-as-voting-option/) to vote, [**stay**](https://iga.in.gov/legislative/2025/bills/house/1679/details) on the voter rolls, and [**cast**](https://indianacapitalchronicle.com/2025/02/03/measure-halving-early-voting-period-heads-to-senate-floor/) early in-person ballots.
- Elections officials in [**Iowa**](https://www.desmoinesregister.com/story/news/politics/2025/02/03/secretary-of-state-boots-183000-iowans-off-voter-rolls-for-inactivity/78186958007/) and Arizona’s most populous [**county**](https://www.fox10phoenix.com/news/maricopa-county-removes-voters-from-registration-list-heres-why) have removed thousands allegedly inactive voters from the rolls as a new election cycle begins.
- In good news, [**Mississippi**](https://mississippitoday.org/2025/02/05/measures-allowing-former-felons-to-regain-voting-rights-clear-house-committees/) and [**Virginia**](https://boltsmag.org/virginia-advances-measure-to-end-lifetime-ban-on-voting/) lawmakers have proposed reforms to their states’ stringent voting rights restorations process for individuals with previous felony convictions

Yes, we are watching Donald Trump attack democracy by undermining governmental institutions and violating the law and Constitution. But we are also watching something else that might be even bigger. We are watching the party he leads attack the right to vote across in a multitude of ways that are not always easy to detect.

That is the current state of democracy.
